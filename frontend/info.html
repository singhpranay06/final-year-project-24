Information Related Blood parameter

1. Age

As individuals age, the risk of cardiovascular issues tends to increase.

2. Sex

Gender plays a role in heart health, with variations in risk factors. For example, men generally face a higher risk of heart disease at a younger age, while women may experience an increased risk after menopause.

3. Resting BP (Blood Pressure)

Resting blood pressure is a crucial indicator of heart health. High blood pressure, or hypertension, strains the heart and arteries, increasing the risk of heart disease and stroke.

4. Cholesterol

Cholesterol levels impact heart health, with high levels of LDL ("bad") cholesterol and low levels of HDL ("good") cholesterol increasing the risk of atherosclerosis. This condition involves the buildup of fatty deposits in the arteries, restricting blood flow and potentially leading to heart attacks or strokes.

5. Fasting BS (Blood Sugar)

Elevated fasting blood sugar levels, indicative of diabetes or insulin resistance, are associated with an increased risk of heart disease. High blood sugar damages blood vessels over time, contributing to atherosclerosis and other cardiovascular complications.

6. Resting ECG (Electrocardiogram)

Resting ECG provides information about the heart's electrical activity. Abnormalities in the ECG, such as irregular rhythms or signs of previous heart attacks, can indicate underlying heart conditions, helping to assess the overall heart health of an individual.

7. Max HR (Maximum Heart Rate)

Maximum heart rate is an important parameter during exercise stress testing. It helps evaluate cardiovascular fitness and provides insights into the heart's ability to respond to physical stress. Regular exercise that aligns with one's maximum heart rate can enhance heart health and overall well-being.

8. Exercise Angina

Exercise-induced angina, chest pain or discomfort during physical activity, may signal inadequate blood flow to the heart. This symptom requires attention, as it can indicate underlying coronary artery disease and an increased risk of heart events.

9. Oldpeak

Oldpeak, or the ST depression during exercise, is an ECG change associated with reduced blood flow to the heart. It can be a sign of coronary artery disease and may indicate the severity of the condition. Monitoring oldpeak helps assess the risk of future cardiovascular events.

10. ST Slope

The ST slope on an ECG measures the upward or downward direction of the ST segment. Abnormal ST slope changes during exercise can suggest myocardial ischemia, a condition where the heart muscle receives insufficient blood supply. This is a critical factor in assessing heart health and identifying potential cardiovascular risks.
